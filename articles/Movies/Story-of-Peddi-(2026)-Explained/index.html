When director Buchi Babu Sana announced that Ram Charan would play a character navigating three completely different sports, most people raised an eyebrow. A crossover athlete in a 1990s rural village? Sounds ambitious at best, absurd at worst. And yet, Peddi — released on June 4, 2026 — somehow pulls it off with enough raw emotion and kinetic energy to keep you hooked for most of its three-hour runtime.

The film is set in Vizianagaram district of Andhra Pradesh, in a remote hillside settlement that doesn't officially exist on any government map. The people living there are undocumented — no identity papers, no railway connection, no legal presence whatsoever. Into this world steps Peddi (Ram Charan), a scrappy, fearless young man who uses sport as a means of survival, pride, and eventually, transformation. Peddi Story Explained

The Village That Doesn't Exist

The story opens with a framing device: Boman Irani plays a senior government official from a national sports academy, who arrives in a small Andhra town in the present day. A local man promises to take him on a long trek to a hilltop village to tell him about someone who changed everything. That someone is Peddi.

We flash back to 1990s Vizianagaram. Peddi lives in Konda Kinda Ooru — "the village in the valley" — a nameless settlement clinging to the hills. The people there technically do not exist. They have no documentation, no land records, no government recognition. This shapes everything about who Peddi is: spirited, hungry, and deeply aware that the world has no obligation to care about him or his people.

Peddi the Cricket Mercenary

Peddi is, at the start of the film, a cricketer-for-hire. Two rival local teams — one led by Ram Bujji (Divyenndu Sharma) and the other from Bobbili — compete aggressively for him each season. Peddi usually ends up playing for Ram Bujji, who outbids the competition by a single rupee. Petty, but effective.

Meanwhile, Appalasoori (Jagapathi Babu), a kind elder from Peddi's village, has spent thirty years petitioning government offices to build a railway station near their settlement — a station that would give the village a legal address and its people an official identity. He keeps getting shut out. The bureaucratic cruelty is slow and grinding, and Jagapathi Babu brings a quiet, heartbreaking dignity to this man who refuses to give up.

Key Theme

The film frames identity — not sport — as its true subject. Winning games matters, but it's never really about winning. It's about whether your village gets to exist on a map.

Love, Politics, and a Public Humiliation

Things get complicated when Peddi falls for Achiyamma (Janhvi Kapoor), the daughter of a local politician running against Ram Bujji's father. The political rivalry spills into personal territory, and Peddi eventually finds himself on Ram Bujji's hit list. After a particularly humiliating confrontation on the cricket pitch — engineered specifically to break Peddi's spirit — a significant shift occurs.

Gournaidu (Shiva Rajkumar), a veteran wrestling coach whose Kushti academy is slowly dying out, notices something in Peddi. Not just his physical ability, but his anger — controlled, purposeful, and bottomless. Gournaidu offers Peddi a place in his academy, and Peddi, with few other options and a fire that needs an outlet, accepts.

The Wrestling Arc — and the Interval Twist

The second half opens with Peddi training under Gournaidu. The wrestling sequences are the film's visual highlight — brutal, traditional, and shot with a level of gritty authenticity that makes you feel the weight of every fall. Peddi rises quickly through the ranks, and it looks like the film is building toward a Kushti championship climax.

⚠️ Spoiler Ahead

Just when the wrestling arc seems to be settling into a formula, Peddi realises that Kushti, for all its cultural prestige, isn't going to get his village what it truly needs — legal recognition and government attention at scale.

Peddi Ending Explained

In the film's final stretch, Peddi makes a dramatic pivot — he transitions from wrestling to track and field athletics. The shift surprises everyone around him, including Gournaidu, who feels a complicated mix of pride and bewilderment. But Peddi has done his homework. A national-level athletics event will have direct government observation, live coverage, and the kind of visibility that no local Kushti match can provide.

In the climax, Peddi doesn't just win his race — he wins it in a way that draws direct attention to his nameless village. His victory, combined with Appalasoori's decades-long paperwork trail, finally forces the government machinery to act. The village gets its railway station. More importantly, the people get identity documents. They officially exist.

Appalasoori — who has carried this fight on his back for thirty years — witnesses it all. It's the most emotionally charged moment in the film, and Jagapathi Babu handles it beautifully without overplaying a single second. Ram Charan's Peddi, for once, isn't raising a trophy. He's watching an old man cry with relief, and that's more than enough.

The framing device closes out: Boman Irani's official, having heard the full story, finally understands why he had never heard of Peddi before. Peddi never sought recognition for himself. He was always playing for others.

Ending Meaning

Peddi's transformation across three sports isn't about athletic versatility. Each sport represents a stage in his understanding of what his community actually needs. Cricket gave him survival. Wrestling gave him respect. Athletics gave his village a future.

Final Verdict

🎯 Our Take on Peddi

Peddi is the kind of film that wears its heart on its sleeve, sometimes to a fault. At 189 minutes, it pushes the limit of what a commercial sports drama can hold, and the Janhvi Kapoor track never quite finds its footing within the larger story. But when the film commits to what it does best — Ram Charan's intensity, Jagapathi Babu's quiet heartbreak, and Buchi Babu Sana's eye for authentic rural texture — it genuinely moves you. It's not a perfect film. It is, however, a memorable one.
